## Lesson 17: Reading the Room in an Uber
Category: strangers
Difficulty: beginner

### Scenario
You slide into the back of a Toyota Camry at 9pm on a Friday. The driver, Marcus, has the radio on low — some talk show, not music. You've got a 12-minute ride ahead. You don't want to sit in silence if the guy's friendly, but you also don't want to be that passenger who won't shut up. The question is whether Marcus wants company or just wants to drive.

### READ — What's going on?
Question: What does the radio choice tell you about Marcus's state right now?
A) He's bored and desperately wants someone to talk to.
B) He's probably neutral — the radio is ambient, not a strong signal either way.
C) He's clearly in a bad mood and wants to be left alone.
D) He's trying to fill the silence because he's nervous about the ride.

Correct: B
Explanation: Talk radio at low volume is ambient noise, not a statement. It doesn't signal "talk to me" or "leave me alone." Marcus hasn't made a move either way. That's your cue to send a light probe and read what comes back.

### THINK — What do they need from you?
Question: What's the right move in the first 30 seconds?
A) Ask a direct question that requires a real answer, so you can gauge his energy.
B) Stay completely silent and let him lead.
C) Drop a single casual opener and read the length and warmth of his response before deciding anything.
D) Compliment his driving or his car to break the ice warmly.

Correct: C
Explanation: One low-stakes opener is your instrument. You're not actually trying to start a conversation yet, you're testing the water. His response tells you everything. Short and flat means he's in work mode. If he elaborates or asks something back, you've got green light.

### SPEAK — What do you say?
Question: Pick the best opening line.
A) "So do you do this full-time or is it a side thing?"
B) "Busy night?"
C) "How long have you been driving for Uber?"
D) "Nice car, is it comfortable for long shifts?"

Correct: B

### Explanations per answer:
A) This is a personal question that requires him to make a decision about how much to share. If he's not in a talking mood, it creates mild social friction right away. It also implies you're curious about his situation, which some drivers find intrusive.
B) "Busy night?" is dead simple. One word answer means he's not feeling it. Two sentences means he's got something to say. You learn everything you need and he doesn't feel pressured either way.
C) Same problem as A — it's backstory-level. You're asking for a whole explanation before you've even established that he wants to talk. If the vibe is off, this lands awkward.
D) A compliment about the car sounds forced. Drivers hear ride-quality comments constantly. It doesn't open a real thread and it puts him in the position of having to respond warmly to something he doesn't care about.

## Lesson 18: Someone Cuts You in Line
Category: strangers
Difficulty: intermediate

### Scenario
You're at a crowded coffee shop, 8:15am. You've been standing in line for four minutes. A guy named Derek, mid-30s, backpack on, phone in hand, slides in right in front of you like you're not there. The barista's about to take his order. Two other people are behind you watching. You've got maybe three seconds.

### READ — What's going on?
Question: What's most likely true about Derek right now?
A) He saw you and doesn't care, so he's deliberately disrespecting you.
B) He's oblivious — genuinely didn't register that you were in line.
C) He's testing you to see if you'll say something.
D) He's in a rush and made a calculated decision that it was worth cutting.

Correct: B
Explanation: Most line-cuts are oblivious, not malicious. Derek's got his phone out and his head somewhere else. His body language is distracted, not defiant. Treating this like a power move when it's probably just a mistake changes everything about how you should respond.

### THINK — What do they need from you?
Question: What's the right energy to bring?
A) Visible annoyance — let him feel the social cost without escalating.
B) Calm and matter-of-fact. State the situation like it's an obvious correction, not a confrontation.
C) Friendly and joking, so you don't make it awkward for anyone.
D) Stay silent. It's not worth the social cost of saying something.

Correct: B
Explanation: Calm and matter-of-fact is the move because it assumes good intent while still correcting the situation. You're not angry. You're not passive-aggressive. You're just pointing out a fact. This keeps your composure intact, handles the situation, and doesn't blow up into something bigger than it is.

### SPEAK — What do you say?
Question: Pick the best response.
A) "Excuse me, I think there's a line here."
B) "Hey, I think I was actually next."
C) "No worries, but I was here first."
D) "Did you not see me standing here?"

Correct: B

### Explanations per answer:
A) "I think there's a line here" is too vague and slightly passive. It implies he might not have known about the line in general, which is condescending. It also softens the statement enough that he might just nod and stay put.
B) "Hey, I think I was actually next." Short. Direct. No anger in it. The word "actually" does the work — it signals a correction without sounding combative. Most people will immediately step back and apologize. You've handled it and preserved the interaction.
C) "No worries" immediately undercuts you. You're saying it's fine before you've even addressed it. He gets to cut and you've told him it's okay. The correction that follows lands weak.
D) This is a trap. Even said calmly, "did you not see me" puts him on defense. Now he either has to admit fault or push back. You've turned a simple correction into a mini-conflict.

## Lesson 19: Asking to Work In at the Gym
Category: strangers
Difficulty: intermediate

### Scenario
It's 6:30pm at a busy gym. You want to use the squat rack and the only one open has a guy named Chris using it — he's got earbuds in, chalked hands, clearly mid-session. He just racked the bar and is standing there catching his breath. You need the rack. You've got two options: wait indefinitely or ask to work in.

### READ — What's going on?
Question: What's Chris's state right now?
A) Annoyed that anyone would interrupt him — earbuds in means do not disturb.
B) Open to conversation but not going to initiate it.
C) Briefly available — he's between sets and his attention is momentarily free.
D) Territorial about the rack and likely to say no.

Correct: C
Explanation: The moment he racked the bar and stepped back is the window. His focus broke. He's breathing, maybe checking his phone or form in the mirror. This is the exact moment to approach, not while he's mid-lift, not while he's chalking up for the next set.

### THINK — What do they need from you?
Question: What does Chris need from you to say yes comfortably?
A) A quick explanation of your whole workout plan so he knows you won't be hogging it.
B) A short, specific ask that's easy to answer and signals you know gym etiquette.
C) Some friendly small talk first to warm him up before asking.
D) A polite compliment about his lift to put him in a good mood.

Correct: B
Explanation: Gym culture values brevity and directness. Chris doesn't need context. He needs to hear that you know the drill — you'll swap out, you'll work around his sets, you won't mess with his setup. A tight, specific ask communicates all of that without saying it.

### SPEAK — What do you say?
Question: Pick the best thing to say when you walk up.
A) "Hey man, awesome lifts — mind if I jump in real quick?"
B) "Sorry to interrupt, but would it be okay if I worked in between your sets?"
C) "Mind if I work in? How many sets you got left?"
D) "You using this the whole time or can I grab a set while you rest?"

Correct: C

### Explanations per answer:
A) The compliment is unnecessary and reads as sycophantic. "Real quick" also undersells it — if you need multiple sets, you're not being honest about the ask. He might say yes expecting one set and then feel trapped.
B) "Sorry to interrupt" immediately signals you think you're being a burden. You're not — working in is completely normal. Starting with an apology makes the whole thing feel bigger than it is.
C) "Mind if I work in? How many sets you got left?" does two things at once. The first question handles the ask. The second one gets you logistics and opens a thread. Now you're having a brief, natural conversation about actual lifting. That's how gym rapport gets built.
D) The phrasing "grab a set while you rest" sounds like you're trying to claim time that's his. It creates a slight adversarial framing — his rest vs. your set. Working in is collaborative, not competitive.

## Lesson 20: The Barista Having a Bad Day
Category: strangers
Difficulty: advanced

### Scenario
You walk into a coffee shop around 2pm on a Tuesday. The place is quiet now but clearly slammed an hour ago — there's a stack of cups still to be rinsed, a wet rag sitting on the counter, and the barista, a woman named Jess, is moving fast and not making eye contact when she greets you. She rattles off "what can I get you" before you've fully reached the counter. Nothing rude, but zero warmth.

### READ — What's going on?
Question: What's most likely true about Jess right now?
A) She's had a difficult customer earlier and is guarded against everyone.
B) She's running on fumes after a rush and is in pure transaction mode.
C) She's unhappy in her job generally and this is just how she is.
D) She's irritated with you specifically for coming in during a slow moment.

Correct: B
Explanation: The physical cues tell the story. Uncleaned cups, wet rag, clipped greeting — this is post-rush fatigue, not hostility. She's still in her head from the chaos. She's not guarded, she's just not fully present yet. That's a different read and it matters for what you do next.

### THINK — What do they need from you?
Question: What's the right move here?
A) Be extra warm and upbeat to counterbalance her energy and lift the interaction.
B) Keep it purely transactional — give your order, say thanks, and let her breathe.
C) Notice something specific about her situation and say something brief and genuine, then read whether she wants to go further.
D) Ask how her day's going to give her an opening to vent if she wants.

Correct: C
Explanation: The advanced skill here isn't just being nice. It's noticing what's actually true and naming it in a way that's human, not performative. "You look like you've been running nonstop" lands differently than "hope your day gets better." One is observational and real. The other is a pleasantry. After you say it, you pause. If she laughs or says something back, she wants a moment of connection. If she half-smiles and keeps moving, she just wants to get through the transaction, and you let her.

### SPEAK — What do you say?
Question: Pick the best thing to say after you've given your order.
A) "Looks like it was a crazy rush earlier, huh? You doing okay?"
B) "You look like you've been running nonstop today."
C) "Hope your day gets better, you deserve a break."
D) "Man, I don't know how you guys handle the lunch rush."

Correct: B

### Explanations per answer:
A) Two questions back to back puts mild pressure on her to respond. "You doing okay" also invites a longer answer than she might want to give in the middle of her shift. It's warmer than silence but it slightly overshoots.
B) "You look like you've been running nonstop today." It's an observation, not a question. She doesn't have to respond. If she does, great, you go from there. If she gives a tired nod and a smile, that's a complete interaction — you saw her, she felt it, no friction. That's the whole point.
C) "You deserve a break" is well-meaning but vaguely condescending. It's also generic, the kind of thing people say without really looking at the other person. It slides off instead of landing.
D) This one's close. It acknowledges what she went through. But "I don't know how you guys handle it" frames her as a category ("you guys" = service workers) rather than a person. It reads as slightly performative empathy rather than genuine recognition.
