# Platform-specific handling and conversion nuances

## The three platforms are not interchangeable

**TikTok** is the Interest Graph at its purest. Sound-driven discovery. Trending audio is algorithmic leverage. Captions function as search — Gary Vaynerchuk's team has shown that "POV: you're feeling lazy and don't feel like doing anything" as a caption drove 12.9M views on an 11-second video by @jun_yuh because the caption matched a search query. Duet and green-screen formats stack curiosity (the viewer has to know what the creator is reacting to).

**Instagram Reels** rewards **shares over views**. VaynerMedia's HALLS × Maya & Hunter campaign hit 10M+ organic views (8.3x their combined audience) driven by 17,000+ shares. For Reels, your hook has to be screenshottable or quotable — Jillian Turecki and Nedra Tawwab dominate here because their aphoristic single-sentence openers are made for Story re-shares. Also: trending audio on IG Reels is often different from TikTok for the same content — cross-platform creators like Chipotle have had to re-cut videos with platform-native sounds.

**YouTube Shorts** is governed by Paddy Galloway's **VVSA metric** (Viewed vs. Swiped Away). The 70-90% VVSA band is viral territory. Shorts also have longer effective length (50-60 seconds averages 1.7M views), lower RPM (~$0.06/1000), and weaker sub conversion (16.9/10K) than long-form — but for sub-1M-channel creators, Shorts are the acquisition funnel that feeds long-form.

**Tactical conclusion for Social IQ:** Start TikTok-first (Gen Z 18-25 primary platform), cross-post to Reels with different trending audio, use Shorts as a third-string distribution with longer variants (50-60s). Don't expect 1:1 performance across platforms for the same cut.

## Hooks that go viral vs. hooks that convert

This is the distinction you specifically asked for, and the literature is unambiguous.

**Hooks that go viral but don't convert:**

- Pure spectacle (Haley Kalil's Met Gala). Massive views, near-zero brand equity or downstream conversion. Reach ≠ revenue.
- Comedy roasting (Drew Afualo's red-flag list). 8M+ followers, but conversion is to podcast listening and book, not to products.
- Controversial confessionals (Kendra Hilty). Tens of millions of views, mostly negative brand effect, unstable conversion.
- Aesthetic-only GRWMs without payload.

**Hooks that convert to installs/subscriptions/sales:**

- The **personalized decoder** (Hussey's AskMH.com, Sabrina's quiz, Dr. Julie's book): hook promises to decode HER situation specifically.
- **Named frameworks** that become vocabulary (Let Them, High Five, Box Theory): the name travels even when the creator doesn't.
- **Screenshottable scripts** (Hussey's wife-text quote, Turecki's *"Repeat after me…"*): the viewer saves a specific line she'll use IRL.
- **Quiz funnels** (Sabrina Zohar's *"How Do You Love?"* quiz → email → course; Hussey's *Dating Made Simple* free masterclass → email → product).

**The rule of thumb:** If the hook promises to change HER internal state ("feel better about your anxious attachment"), you get views. If the hook promises to change HER external world ("know what to say at the interview Friday"), you get installs.

**The position: "I'm a guy who studies social dynamics. I explain what men actually do and why."**

Visibly desirable on camera, never spoken about. ## Why a male creator works for this demo specifically

Women 18-25 are saturated with female dating-advice creators. Hussey, the exception, converts harder than any of them because he offers something the female creators structurally cannot: insider access to male psychology from the source. A woman telling women what men think is a theory. A man telling women what men think is a report from the other side.

This is the honest source of credibility for a male creator in this space. Not "I became successful with women and now I'll teach you." Not "I used to fail and here's what I fixed." Both of those structures, regardless of how carefully framed, pattern-match to pickup-artist content for a smart female viewer. The only safe credibility is: "I'm a guy. I notice patterns. Here's one."

## Permanent content rules

Never reference your own attractiveness or desirability. Never reference how women respond to you. Never position yourself as having solved dating. No before/after framing involving your own dating life. Nothing that could be screenshotted out of context and read as manosphere content.

The protagonist of your content is the pattern, never you. In Hussey's wife-text video, his wife is the protagonist and he's the witness. The fact that he landed her is implied but never stated. That's the move.

## What ships

Decoding male behavior for women: what he means when he does X, what he's actually thinking in Y situation, why guys do Z. This is the conversion-pillar content.

Reverse-engineering socially skilled moves you've witnessed women make: "a woman I was out with did X, here's why it worked." Protagonist is her skill.

Mechanism explanations for dating and social patterns most people miss.

## The app evolution this commits to

Social IQ moves, over the next 3-6 months, toward a dating-decoder positioning. New lessons built in this window weight heavily toward dating scenarios and male-behavior decoding. Existing workplace/family/friendship lessons stay, but they're not the growth surface.

Think "AskMH.com as an iOS app for 18-25" as the product direction. A woman installs expecting to decode the guy she's currently seeing. The app delivers on that within the first lesson. Workplace and family content becomes secondary depth, not the leading edge.

## The three content formulas to run against this positioning

Formula one, the Hussey confession-plus-script. Open with a specific male behavior or an exact text/line. Decode what he meant. Give the viewer a verbatim script she can screenshot. This is the highest-conversion format in the entire research library.

Formula two, the Zohar negative-imperative. "Stop doing X when a guy does Y. Here's what's actually happening." Calls out a specific female behavior at the moment she's doing it (evening phone-scroll), reframes generously, delivers insider male perspective on why her read was wrong.

Formula three, the reverse-engineered witness. "A girl I was on a date with did [specific thing]. Here's why it worked on me." Protagonist is her skill. Viewer takes the move.

Thirty videos against these three formulas before any novelty. That's the discipline.

## The CTA pattern that converts

Not "download Social IQ to learn social skills." Instead: "download Social IQ, tell it about the specific guy or situation you're dealing with, get a tailored read on what's happening and what to do." The decoder-in-your-pocket fantasy is the install driver.

The first lesson the app auto-starts should be generated from the user's onboarding answers about her current situation, not the same generic first lesson for every user. That's the conversion moment — the reel promised personalized decoding and the app delivers it within 30 seconds of install.
